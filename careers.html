Nursing & Allied Healthcare professionals

The most trusted profession in America

Nurses at all levels and in all health care settings have played a critical role in the nation’s continued fight against COVID-19. With approximately 4.2 million registered nurses and over 325,000 licensed nurse practitioners, the nursing profession continues to flourish. According to the University of California at San Francisco, more than 85% of California hospitals report that demand for RNs is greater than supply.

Nurses have been identified as the most honest and ethical professionals for the past 19 years with an 89% of the vote in 2020.

- Gallup


For the last 20 years, nursing has been ranked as the most trusted profession in the United States. As the demand grows across the globe to provide care and support for aged care, mental health, disability and other health sectors, the demand for nursing and Allied Health workforce also increases.

Most Trusted in America
Allied health professionals comprise nearly 60% of the healthcare workforce.
There are 4x as many nurses in the United States as there are physicians.
Demand for allied health workers is high — 73% of the counties in Texas are designated as “Health Professions Shortage Areas.”

There are currently 4 million registered nurses in the United States alone however, thanks to the demands of an increasingly complex healthcare industry the new landscape role of nurses and allied healthcare professionals has evolved. With roles ranging from direct patient care and case management to establishing nursing standards, developing procedures and complex nursing systems, nursing makes the front-line staff with a time-honored reputation for compassion and dedication.

Nursing & Allied Healthcare Professionals

Who are they?
There are more than five million allied health care providers in the United States, working in more than 80 different professions and representing approximately 60% of all health care providers.

The following are included in Allied Health:
  • Athletic training
  • Audiology
  • Cardiovascular perfusion technology
  • Cytotechnology
  • Dental hygiene
  • Diagnostic medical sonography
  • Dietetics
  • Emergency medical sciences
  • Health administration
  • Health information management
  • Medical technology
  • Nuclear medicine technology
  • Occupational therapy
  • Physical therapy
  • Physician assistant
  • Radiation therapy technology
  • Radiography
  • Rehabilitation counseling
  • Respiratory therapy
  • Respiratory therapy technology
  • Speech-language pathology

While all nurses complete a rigorous program of education, they all work directly with patients, families and communities. Nursing in the United States is divided under these three separate banners by the specific responsibilities they undertake:

  • Registered Nurses (RN)
  • Advanced Practice Registered Nurses (APRN)
  • Licensed Practical Nurses (LPN)
